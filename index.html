Matthew Wolak

I'm a biologist endeavouring to discover and describe differences among individuals within a population in the way that they look, behave, and perform. The goal of my research is to measure genetic and environmental factors that create variability in a population. Armed with these measurements, I can then make quantitative predictions about changes in the distribution of trait values within and between generations. My results contribute to general scientific knowledge regarding the way organisms interact with their environments (where the environment can mean a harsh winter, a predator, or even the behavior of a neighbor) and the genetic and non-genetic inheritance of characteristics across time and generations. My contributions have been applied (both by myself and by others) to the conservation of threatened or endagered species, agricultural breeding practices, and human medical practices.

My research programe employs several complementary approaches that allow me to integrate techniques and ideas from different fields of research into my own work. I study a variety of organisms in several different settings:

  • completely un-managed, natural populations
  • wild caught individuals that are used for laboratory breeding designs
  • artificially selected lines of laboratory populations

I also use computer simulations to study biology in silico, from which I create and test theory. Computer simulation is also an important means by which I test current statistical methods for their ability to accurately address biological hypotheses. If these methods are found inappropriate or hard to implement, I develop statistical methods and software to be used by other researchers.

On these pages, you can read more about specific research projects, software I have created or am currently developing, or my previous experience and accomplishments.
